Question

Some people think that good health is very important to every person, so medical service should not be run by profit-making companies.


Do the advantages of private healthcare outweigh the disadvantages?


Give reasons for your answer and include any relevant examples from your knowledge and experience.

Write at least 250 words.

Sample Answer

Healthcare, beyond question, is a basic human right, ensuring that it should be available for everyone. A common belief is that the importance of health is unrivaled for every person and so suggests that profit-seeking companies should not operate medical services. I am firmly in agreement with this stance.

The weightiest argument countering private healthcare is the inherent danger of creating societal disparities. When managed by profit-making companies, healthcare services may become a luxury only affordable by the wealthy. It's both a moral and social obligation that healthcare shouldn't be a product for sale to the highest paying individual. The circumstances of one's birth shouldn't determine the care they receive.


In addition, privatization presents severe challenges about the priority of health service providers. Unlike public health services committed to providing care to all impartially, private entities may prioritize patients who can pay more and treatments that yield a higher profit. This bias, evident in running healthcare on free-market norms, threatens the principal idea of healthcare as a service open to all.


Another counterpoint against privatization is that it could fragment the unity in public health. Private firms are prone to focus on high-profit zones, likely leaving less developed rural areas or poorer regions underserved. This judicious selection directly challenges public health's mission.


Furthermore, the motivation for profit might unintentionally shift the focus toward treatment rather than preventative measures. Though preventative health actions usually save more cost in the long run, they might be ignored if the spotlight is on immediately profitable treatments.


To conclude, while privatization may introduce initial efficiency and investment injections, the potentially negative impacts on the long-term public health interest are too clear to overlook. The private sector, motivated by profit, might divide the healthcare system into wealthy areas and large areas of neglect. Therefore, healthcare, a necessary right, must not be at the mercy of unpredictable market forces and should be governed by the ideals of accessibility, equality, and collective benefit.
