Question

It is impossible to help all people around the world in need, so governments should focus on helping people in their own countries.


To what extent do you agree or disagree?


Give reasons for your answer and include any relevant examples from your knowledge and experience.

Write at least 250 words.

Sample Answer

In the realm of social and political debates, there is a heated debate about whether governments should primarily concentrate on assisting those within their borders, given the magnitude and diversity of global needs. Although many argue that resources should be reserved for national citizens, I believe in a balanced approach that takes into account both domestic and international needs.

Undeniably, the government’s primary responsibility is to cater to its constituents. In countless nations, especially in developing ones, citizens struggle with poverty, malnutrition, lack of education, and inadequate healthcare facilities. As the guardians of their sovereign territory, governments are ethically obliged to deploy available funds and resources to improve these plaguing conditions before reaching across borders.

However, we must acknowledge that we live in a deeply interconnected world where national boundaries often blur in the face of shared challenges such as climate change, disease outbreaks or even geopolitical conflicts. During the COVID-19 pandemic, for instance, numerous countries offered each other material and technical support, underscoring the necessity of 'global citizenship'. Moreover, aid given to strife-torn or impoverished countries often mitigates refugee crises and indirectly promotes economic and political stability in the helping countries.

Citing my own country as an example, we have significantly benefitted from international help during natural calamities. At the same time, we have extended our assistance to others in their times of tragedy. This mutual aid system carves out a path for mutual understanding and empathy, ensuring that a hand will always be reachable in times of despair.

To conclude, though it is pragmatic for government aid to be primarily directed towards their own citizens due to limited resources, it is not feasible to completely overlook global obligations, particularly in a world that is more connected than ever. It’s an intricate balancing act, a dance between 'me' and 'we'. By doing so, we can aspire to build nations that are not only independent but also compassionate global citizens.
