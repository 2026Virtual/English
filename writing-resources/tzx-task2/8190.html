Question

Some people think that in order to prevent illness and disease, governments should make efforts in reducing environmental pollutions and housing problems. 


To what extent do you agree to disagree with this statement? 

Give reasons for your answer and include any relevant examples from your knowledge and experience.

Write at least 250 words.

Sample Answer

Some people argue that governments can prevent many illnesses by tackling environmental pollution and housing problems. Although improving these conditions is certainly beneficial for public health, I believe that relying on them alone is not enough. A broader range of measures is required to effectively reduce disease.


Reducing environmental pollution is an important step towards improving public health. Air pollution, for example, has been linked to asthma, heart disease and various respiratory infections. When governments impose stricter controls on industrial emissions and promote cleaner forms of transport, the quality of the air people breathe naturally improves. Living in a cleaner environment also encourages people to spend more time outdoors, which can support both physical and mental well-being. Additionally, tackling poor housing conditions is equally important. Damp, overcrowded or poorly ventilated homes can worsen allergies and spread infectious diseases more easily. Ensuring that all citizens have access to safe and stable housing can reduce these health risks significantly.


However, while these measures are valuable, they do not address many of the most common causes of modern illness. Lifestyle-related diseases such as obesity, diabetes and heart problems are often the result of poor diet, lack of exercise and high stress levels. These issues cannot be solved simply by improving the environment or housing. Governments therefore need to invest in wider public health strategies. These include offering regular health checks, funding community sports facilities, and launching campaigns to educate people about healthy eating and disease prevention. In many cases, simple changes such as reducing sugar consumption or increasing weekly exercise can have a greater impact on health than environmental improvements alone.


In conclusion, reducing pollution and improving housing can help prevent certain illnesses, but these actions are not sufficient on their own. A combination of environmental policies and direct public health initiatives is necessary if governments truly aim to reduce disease and promote long-term well-being.
